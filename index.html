Steps for Dealing with Compromised Items

1Immediate Isolation

The compromised item (or the entire load, if the compromise affects the whole batch) must be immediately isolated from all other sterile or clean items.

It should be clearly marked as "compromised" or "non-sterile."

2Do Not Use

Under no circumstances should a compromised item be used on a patient. Its sterility cannot be guaranteed.

3Dismantle for Reprocessing

If the item is a multi-part instrument, it must be dismantled into its individual components. This is crucial because contaminants can hide in crevices, hinges, or internal channels, and sterilising agents may not penetrate effectively if the item is not fully disassembled.

Dismantling ensures that all surfaces can be thoroughly cleaned and that the sterilant can reach every part during the subsequent reprocessing cycle.

4Full Reprocessing

The dismantled components must re-enter the complete reprocessing cycle from the very beginning. This includes:

  • Cleaning: Thorough manual and/or automated cleaning to remove all organic material and debris.
  • Inspection: Detailed inspection of each component for cleanliness, damage, and functionality.
  • Packaging: Re-packaging the item correctly in appropriate sterilisation packaging.
  • Sterilisation: Running the item through a new, validated sterilisation cycle.

5Documentation of Failure

All details of the compromised item and the failed sterilisation event must be meticulously recorded. This includes:

  • The date and time of identification.
  • The specific reason for compromise (e.g., "damp pack," "torn bag," "indicator fail").
  • The steriliser number and cycle/load number.
  • Details of the item(s) affected.
  • The corrective action taken (i.e., reprocessing).
  • The name and signature of the person identifying and documenting the issue.

This documentation is vital for quality assurance, auditing, and identifying potential recurring issues with equipment or procedures.

6Inform Supervisor/Manager

The incident must be reported to a supervisor or manager to ensure awareness, facilitate investigation into the cause, and implement any necessary systemic changes to prevent future occurrences.

7Quarantine and Investigation

If a pattern of compromised items emerges, or if the failure is significant (e.g., a steriliser malfunction), the steriliser may need to be quarantined and investigated by a qualified technician before further use.
